# Collect a compehensive list of cognitive biases that includes name, description, and brief example. Make the list as complete as possible.

Psychologists have documented well over 180 named cognitive biases, so no single list is literally complete, but the biases below cover most of the major, repeatedly cited ones in modern taxonomies.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

***

## How to read this list

Each entry gives the bias name, a one‑sentence description, and a one‑sentence concrete example. The list is grouped loosely by theme (judgment, memory, social, etc.), but many biases could fit multiple categories.[^2][^3][^1]

***

## General judgment and belief biases

- **Confirmation bias** – Tendency to seek, notice, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs while discounting disconfirming evidence.[^5][^6][^1]
Example: A person who believes a supplement works only remembers times they felt better after taking it and ignores times it did nothing.[^6][^5]
- **Belief bias** – Evaluating arguments based on how believable the conclusion feels rather than whether the reasoning is logically valid.[^1][^2]
Example: Someone accepts a logically flawed argument because it supports their political view, but rejects a logically sound argument that opposes it.[^2][^1]
- **Congruence bias** – Tendency to test only the hypothesis we already have in mind instead of considering alternatives.[^7][^1]
Example: A manager convinced sales are low due to price only experiments with discounts, never testing whether marketing or product quality is the issue.[^7]
- **Motivated reasoning** – Tendency to reason toward conclusions we *want* to be true, treating desired conclusions more leniently than undesired ones.[^8][^2]
Example: An investor searches harder for flaws in reports that suggest selling a favorite stock than in reports that suggest buying more.[^8][^2]
- **Bias blind spot** – Seeing biases in others more readily than in oneself.[^1][^7]
Example: A doctor easily spots confirmation bias in colleagues’ diagnoses but insists their own judgments are strictly evidence‑based.[^9][^1]
- **Naïve realism** – Belief that one’s own view is simply “seeing the world as it is,” so those who disagree are biased or misinformed.[^2]
Example: Two people with opposite views on a news story each conclude that the other side is irrational, not that the story is ambiguous.[^2]
- **Illusory truth effect** – Repeated statements feel more true merely because they are familiar.[^3][^1]
Example: After hearing the same catchy but false health claim many times online, a person starts to accept it as fact.[^3][^1]
- **Anchoring bias** – Relying too heavily on the first number or piece of information encountered when making decisions.[^6][^7][^1]
Example: If the first price quoted for a used car is 15,000 dollars, later counteroffers feel reasonable only in comparison to that anchor.[^3][^6]
- **Framing effect** – Different choices are made depending on whether options are presented as gains or losses, even when outcomes are equivalent.[^5][^6][^1]
Example: People are more likely to accept a surgery when told it has a “90% survival rate” than when told it has a “10% mortality rate.”[^6]
- **Status quo bias** – Preference for keeping things as they are, even when change would likely be better.[^1][^3]
Example: Employees stick with an old, inefficient software system simply because it is familiar.[^3]
- **Omission bias** – Judging harmful actions as worse than equally harmful failures to act.[^5][^1]
Example: Someone considers a doctor who gives a risky treatment more blameworthy than one who withholds it, even if both decisions cause the same harm.[^5]

## Probability, risk, and decision biases

- **Availability heuristic** – Judging likelihood by how easily examples come to mind, which overweights vivid, recent, or emotional events.[^6][^2][^3]
Example: After seeing news about plane crashes, a person becomes more afraid of flying than of driving despite the statistics.[^2][^6]
- **Representativeness heuristic** – Judging probabilities by how much something resembles a stereotype, ignoring base rates.[^1][^2]
Example: Assuming a shy, bookish person is more likely to be a librarian than a salesperson, even though there are far more salespeople.[^2]
- **Base rate neglect (base rate fallacy)** – Ignoring the general prevalence of an event when evaluating specific evidence.[^7][^1]
Example: Believing a positive result on a rare‑disease test almost guarantees illness without considering that false positives are common when the disease is rare.[^7][^1]
- **Conjunction fallacy** – Mistakenly thinking detailed, specific scenarios are more probable than simpler, more general ones.[^7][^1]
Example: Calling it more likely that “Linda is a feminist bank teller” than that “Linda is a bank teller.”[^1]
- **Neglect of probability** – Focusing on outcomes and emotions while disregarding their actual probabilities.[^8][^1]
Example: Refusing a medical treatment because of a rare side effect, even when the chance of benefit is far higher.[^1]
- **Gambler’s fallacy** – Believing past random events change the odds of future ones.[^3][^1]
Example: Thinking a coin is “due” to land heads after several tails in a row.[^1]
- **Hot‑hand fallacy** – Believing a run of success will continue in a random process.[^5][^1]
Example: Assuming a basketball player will keep scoring because they have made their last few shots.[^1]
- **Optimism bias** – Overestimating the likelihood of positive outcomes for oneself.[^6][^5][^1]
Example: A driver believes they are less likely than average to have an accident, despite similar driving habits.[^5][^6]
- **Pessimism bias** – Overestimating the likelihood of negative outcomes.[^5][^1]
Example: A student expects to fail an exam despite consistently earning good grades.[^5]
- **Planning fallacy** – Underestimating how long tasks will take, even when one has experience of similar tasks running late.[^6][^5][^1]
Example: A team schedules two weeks for a software feature that ends up taking six.[^6]
- **Hyperbolic discounting / present bias** – Heavily favoring immediate rewards over larger, delayed ones.[^5][^1]
Example: Choosing 20 dollars today instead of 30 dollars in a month.[^5]
- **Loss aversion** – Losses feel more painful than equivalent gains feel pleasant.[^10][^1]
Example: People are more upset about losing 100 dollars than they are pleased about gaining 100 dollars.[^1]
- **Outcome bias** – Judging a decision by its result rather than by whether the decision was reasonable at the time.[^9][^1]
Example: Calling a medically justified treatment choice “bad” only because the patient had complications.[^9]
- **Hindsight bias (“I knew it all along”)** – After an outcome is known, seeing it as having been predictable.[^2][^6]
Example: After a company fails, investors say it was “obvious” from the start, despite their earlier optimism.[^6]

## Memory and attention biases

- **Misinformation effect** – Memories are distorted by misleading information encountered after the event.[^6][^1]
Example: After hearing a leading question about “the car smashing,” a witness recalls a collision as more severe than it was.[^6]
- **Peak–end rule** – Remembering an experience mostly by its most intense point and the end, not by the average of all moments.[^1][^5]
Example: A long, mildly uncomfortable procedure that ends gently is recalled more positively than a shorter but sharply painful one that ends badly.[^5]
- **Rosy retrospection** – Remembering past events as more pleasant than they felt at the time.[^1][^5]
Example: Someone recalls a stressful backpacking trip as idyllic years later.[^5]
- **Recency effect** – More recent items are remembered better than earlier ones in a sequence.[^3]
Example: On a long list of names, a person most easily recalls the last few.[^3]
- **Primacy effect** – Earlier items in a sequence are remembered better than middle ones.[^3]
Example: People remember the first candidate in a debate more clearly than those in the middle.[^3]
- **Attentional bias** – Attention is captured by certain types of stimuli (e.g., threats, rewards, cues related to current concerns).[^7][^3]
Example: A person anxious about illness notices every cough in a crowded room.[^3]
- **Frequency illusion (Baader–Meinhof phenomenon)** – After noticing something once, it suddenly seems to appear everywhere.[^3][^1]
Example: After learning a new word, someone starts hearing it in every conversation and article.[^3]
- **Mere exposure effect** – Repeated exposure to something increases liking for it, even without conscious awareness.[^1][^3]
Example: A song initially disliked becomes enjoyable after being heard many times on the radio.[^3]
- **Fading affect bias** – Negative emotions associated with memories fade faster than positive ones.[^3]
Example: Over time, the joy of a graduation is remembered more vividly than the stress leading up to it.[^3]
- **Suggestibility / false memory bias** – People can form memories of events that never occurred when exposed to suggestions.[^3]
Example: After repeated questioning, a person becomes convinced they saw a detail at a crime scene that was only mentioned by others.[^3]

## Social and attribution biases

- **Fundamental attribution error** – Overemphasizing personality and underestimating situational factors when explaining others’ behavior.[^2][^1]
Example: Assuming a coworker who arrives late is lazy rather than considering traffic or family emergencies.[^2]
- **Actor–observer bias** – Explaining one’s own actions by the situation but others’ identical actions by their character.[^1][^3]
Example: Explaining one’s own speeding as “being rushed,” but others’ speeding as “being reckless.”[^1]
- **Self‑serving bias** – Attributing successes to oneself and failures to external factors.[^5][^1]
Example: A student credits good grades to intelligence but blames poor grades on unfair tests.[^5]
- **In‑group bias** – Favoring members of one’s own group over outsiders.[^1][^3]
Example: A manager consistently rates employees from their own alma mater more positively.[^1]
- **Out‑group homogeneity bias** – Seeing members of other groups as more similar to each other than in‑group members.[^1][^3]
Example: Saying “they’re all the same” about another nationality while noticing wide differences among one’s compatriots.[^3]
- **Halo effect** – A positive impression in one area (e.g., attractiveness) influences judgments in unrelated areas (e.g., competence).[^6][^1]
Example: An attractive job applicant is assumed to be more capable and honest.[^6]
- **Horn effect** – A negative impression in one domain spills over into other judgments.[^1]
Example: A person who makes one clumsy mistake at work is subsequently seen as incompetent in all tasks.[^1]
- **Stereotyping** – Expecting individuals to have traits based solely on group membership.[^2][^1]
Example: Assuming a young person is tech‑savvy regardless of their actual skills.[^1]
- **Prejudice / implicit stereotypes** – Negative or positive attitudes toward groups that operate automatically or unconsciously.[^3]
Example: Automatically feeling less trust toward someone from a stigmatized group despite explicit beliefs in equality.[^3]
- **False consensus effect** – Overestimating how many others share one’s beliefs or behaviors.[^5][^1]
Example: A person who drinks heavily assumes most people drink as much as they do.[^5]
- **Third‑person effect** – Believing others are more influenced by media than oneself.[^3][^1]
Example: Someone claims advertising does not affect them but assumes others are easily manipulated.[^3]
- **Social desirability bias** – Over‑reporting socially approved behaviors and under‑reporting disapproved ones.[^9][^1]
Example: Survey respondents exaggerate how often they exercise and minimize junk‑food consumption.[^9]
- **Bandwagon effect (herd behavior)** – Adopting beliefs or actions because many others do.[^1][^3]
Example: Investing in a trending stock mainly because “everyone is buying it.”[^3]
- **Courtesy bias** – Saying what seems polite or acceptable rather than one’s true opinion.[^7][^1]
Example: Guests tell a host they loved the meal even if they did not.[^7]

## Self, ego, and metacognitive biases

- **Overconfidence effect** – Having unwarranted confidence in judgments, estimates, or skills.[^11][^1]
Example: People are often “99% sure” about answers that turn out to be wrong.[^11]
- **Dunning–Kruger effect** – People with low skill in a domain overestimate their ability, while experts may underestimate theirs.[^6][^5]
Example: A novice chess player believes they are tournament‑ready after a few wins against beginners.[^6]
- **Illusion of control** – Overestimating one’s influence over events that are largely chance‑driven.[^5][^1]
Example: A gambler believes how they throw dice changes the outcome.[^5]
- **Illusion of transparency** – Overestimating how much others can read one’s internal states.[^1][^3]
Example: A nervous speaker assumes the audience can clearly see their anxiety, even when they appear calm.[^3]
- **Spotlight effect** – Overestimating how much others notice one’s appearance or mistakes.[^1][^3]
Example: Someone who spills coffee on their shirt feels everyone is staring, though few notice.[^3]
- **Curse of knowledge** – Difficulty imagining what it is like *not* to know something once one knows it.[^1][^3]
Example: A programmer explains a concept using jargon, forgetting the listener lacks that background.[^3]
- **Self‑consistency bias** – Believing one’s attitudes and behavior are more consistent over time than they actually are.[^3]
Example: A person claims they have “always” held a current political view, forgetting earlier contradictions.[^3]
- **Egocentric bias** – Overemphasizing one’s own role in shared events.[^12]
Example: Two team members each feel they did “most of the work” on a joint project.[^12]
- **Restraint bias** – Overestimating one’s ability to resist temptations or impulses.[^5][^1]
Example: Someone buys junk food believing they will only eat “a small amount,” then overeats.[^5]

***

## Choice, value, and economic biases

- **Endowment effect** – Valuing things more highly simply because one owns them.[^5][^1]
Example: Sellers demand higher prices for an item than they would be willing to pay to buy it.[^5]
- **Status quo bias** – Preference for current options over change, even when alternatives are objectively better (also listed above, but central here).[^1][^3]
Example: Staying with a more expensive bank account to avoid the hassle of switching.[^3]
- **Decoy effect** – Preferences between two options shift when a third, worse “decoy” option is added.[^7]
Example: Adding a clearly inferior subscription plan makes one remaining plan look more attractive.[^7]
- **Default effect** – Strong tendency to accept preset options.[^7]
Example: Organ donation rates are higher where people are donors by default unless they opt out.[^7]
- **Sunk cost fallacy / escalation of commitment** – Continuing a course of action because of past investments rather than current costs and benefits.[^11][^1]
Example: Staying in a failing project because “we’ve already spent so much money on it.”[^11]
- **Denomination effect** – Spending small denominations more freely than equivalent larger denominations.[^7][^3]
Example: People are more willing to spend five 1‑dollar bills than a single 5‑dollar bill.[^3]
- **Time‑saving bias** – Misjudging how much time is saved or lost by speed changes (often underestimating the benefit of improving very low speeds).[^3]
Example: Drivers think increasing speed from 60 to 70 mph saves more time than from 20 to 30 mph over the same distance.[^3]
- **Pro‑innovation bias** – Overvaluing new technologies or ideas simply because they are new.[^3]
Example: Adopting a complex new app even when a simpler existing tool works better.[^3]
- **Money illusion** – Focusing on nominal amounts of money rather than real purchasing power after inflation.[^7][^1]
Example: Feeling richer because of a raise in a high‑inflation period, even though real buying power has not increased.[^1]

***

## Pattern recognition and perception biases

- **Apophenia** – Perceiving meaningful connections in random or unrelated data.[^1]
Example: Seeing a causal pattern in a few coincidental events.[^1]
- **Pareidolia** – Perceiving meaningful images in vague stimuli.[^1][^3]
Example: Seeing faces in clouds or on the surface of the Moon.[^1]
- **Clustering illusion** – Seeing patterns or “streaks” in small samples of random data.[^1][^3]
Example: Believing certain lottery numbers are “hot” because they appeared close together recently.[^3]
- **Illusory correlation** – Believing two variables are related when they are not (or exaggerating a small relationship).[^5][^1]
Example: Assuming full moons cause more hospital emergencies after noticing a few coincidences.[^1]
- **Illusion of validity** – Overconfidence in judgments based on consistent, but actually uninformative, data.[^7][^1]
Example: Feeling sure a personality test predicts job performance because its scales “hang together,” even though it has low predictive power.[^7]
- **Frequency illusion** (also above) – Perceiving something as more common after first noticing it.[^1][^3]
Example: After buying a red car, suddenly noticing red cars everywhere.[^3]
- **Teleological bias** – Over‑attributing purpose or design to events that arose without goal‑directed intention.[^1]
Example: Saying “this illness exists to strengthen our character” as if it has a built‑in purpose.[^1]

***

## Selection and sampling biases

- **Selection bias** – Systematic distortion from using a non‑representative sample.[^9][^1]
Example: Evaluating a training program only on highly motivated volunteers and generalizing results to all employees.[^9]
- **Survivorship bias** – Focusing on successes that “survived” and ignoring failures that are no longer visible.[^5][^1]
Example: Studying only successful companies to learn “what works” while forgetting many firms used similar strategies and failed.[^5]
- **Publication bias** – Studies with positive or surprising results are more likely to be published than null results.[^1]
Example: A treatment seems very effective because negative or inconclusive trials are under‑reported.[^1]
- **Sampling bias / insensitivity to sample size** – Ignoring the importance of sample size in judging variability or reliability.[^7][^1]
Example: Treating a survey of 20 people as equally reliable as one of 2,000.[^7]

***

## Using this list

Researchers continue to add and refine cognitive bias concepts, and visual taxonomies such as the “Cognitive Bias Codex” now map more than 180 distinct named biases into families like “Too much information,” “Not enough meaning,” “Need to act fast,” and “What should we remember?” For deeper exploration or to see many additional, more specialized biases beyond those listed here, comprehensive online catalogues such as Wikipedia’s “List of cognitive biases” and curated libraries like The Decision Lab’s bias index are useful reference points.[^4][^5][^3][^1]
